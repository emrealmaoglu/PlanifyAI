Quantitative Spatial Benchmarking of Key Turkish University Campuses: A Data-Driven Analysis


1.0 Executive Summary: Comparative Spatial Benchmarking

This report presents a comprehensive quantitative analysis and spatial planning benchmark of five leading Turkish universities: Middle East Technical University (METU), Boğaziçi University (BU), Koç University (KU), Istanbul Technical University (ITU), and Bilkent University. The analysis is based exclusively on data extracted from official university master plans, strategic reports, and published academic papers.
The analysis reveals stark contrasts in campus typology. METU operates as a massive regional land reserve, with a total boundary of 4,500 hectares, including a 3,043-hectare forest.1 This "university city" model, whose original master-planned developed area was 800 hectares 3, contrasts sharply with the more compact, integrated campuses of Koç University (160 hectares 5) and Boğaziçi University (191.7 hectares 6). Bilkent University presents a hybrid model with a large suburban campus of approximately 295-500 hectares.7
A primary finding of this report is the significant absence of standardized, quantitative planning metrics such as Floor Area Ratio (FAR) and Building Coverage Ratio (BCR) within the provided university master plans. Planning documents for METU 4 and Bilkent 9 focus on qualitative, principle-based guidelines (e.g., pedestrian-priority, "solid-void ratio" as a concept) rather than the prescriptive quantitative zoning codes requested. Bilkent's own strategic plan notes a history of "ad hoc" development and the explicit need for a master plan to correct this.9
Furthermore, the requested "Yes/No" compliance metrics for accessibility and seismic standards are not available. The data indicates a more complex reality of "compliance in progress":
	•	Accessibility (TS 9111/12576): Universities have established procedural mechanisms, such as Disability Service Offices 10, to mitigate physical accessibility gaps. This is critical, as independent assessments of Ankara campuses describe the physical infrastructure as "deficient and far from TSI standards".12
	•	Seismic Compliance: A critical finding is that while METU, BU, and ITU faculty are national experts in seismic assessment 13, BU and ITU are concurrently part of the World Bank-funded SREEPB project. This project is actively performing seismic strengthening on "high-risk" existing buildings, specifically dormitories.14 This confirms active mitigation of known non-compliance in legacy structures.

3.0 Analysis of Campus Metrics: Land Use, Building Stock, and Green Space


3.1 Total Campus Area and Typology

The data reveals three distinct campus typologies, differentiated by total land area and relationship to the urban fabric.
	•	Regional Land Reserve (METU): METU represents a unique model. Its total land holding is 4,500 hectares (45 km²).1 This vast area is not a "campus" in the traditional sense but a regional land reserve that includes a 3,043-hectare forest and Eymir Lake.1 The original 1961 Çinici master plan, which defined the "university city" concept 3, designated approximately 800 hectares (8 million m²) for the core developed area (buildings, open spaces, and immediate grounds).3 A separate GIS project report identifies the dense academic core as ~100 hectares.21 This stratification (4500 ha total >> 800 ha developed zone >> 100 ha academic core) is critical for spatial planning, as density calculations using the 4,500-hectare denominator would be misleading.
	•	Compact-Suburban (Koç & Boğaziçi): Koç University's main Rumelifeneri campus is 160 hectares.5 Boğaziçi University's total area is 191.7 hectares (1,917,161 m²), distributed across 7 campuses, including the historic 47.7-hectare (118 acres) South Campus.6 These campuses are integrated into the dense urban fabric of Istanbul and are defined by clear, relatively compact boundaries.
	•	Hybrid Model (Bilkent & ITU): Bilkent University occupies a large suburban area, though sources provide conflicting figures of 295.4 hectares 7, 300 hectares 9, and 500 hectares.8 This lack of a single consistent metric may reflect the "ad hoc" development approach mentioned in its own strategic plan.9 Data for ITU's total campus area was not available in the provided sources, though it is noted to have 5 campuses.24

3.2 Building Stock: Count and Footprint

A significant data gap exists in the documentation, with critical ambiguity between "building footprint" (the 2D ground area) and "Gross Floor Area (GFA)" (the 3D sum of all floors).
METU is the only institution with a detailed building count, reporting approximately 450 buildings. This includes a clear functional breakdown: ~63 academic, ~20 administrative, 315 housing units, 15 dormitories, and 19 social buildings.21
For building area, most data refers to GFA, not footprint:
	•	Bilkent University: Reports 537,778 m² of total building area 7, with another source citing 460,000 m².9
	•	Koç University: Provides both metrics, allowing for analysis. It has 199,556 m² of "Indoor Area" (GFA) 22 and a 46,452 m² "Campus/Building footprint".5
	•	ITU: Data is limited to the Civil Engineering Faculty, which has an area of 55,000 m².23
The 800-hectare (8,000,000 m²) figure for METU's "buildings occupy" area 3 appears to reference the total developed zone from the 1961 plan, including buildings, plazas, and pedestrian paths, not the building footprints themselves.

3.3 Green Space Metrics

Green space metrics also reflect the different campus typologies.
	•	METU: 3,043-hectare forest.1
	•	Bilkent: 162.2-hectare forest area.7
	•	ITU: 65-hectare (650,000 m²) "green area".24
Analysis of "Green Space per Capita" reveals the planning implications of these different green space types.
	•	ITU: Explicitly states 25 m² of green space per person.24 Based on its student population of 38,636 18, this 65-hectare area is clearly managed, programmed green space (lawns, quads) integrated within the campus.
	•	METU: A calculation based on its 3,043-hectare forest 1 and 23,395 students 2 yields ~1,300 m² per person.
	•	Bilkent: A calculation based on its 162.2-hectare forest 7 and 12,000 students 19 yields ~135 m² per person.
For a spatial planner, METU's and Bilkent's metrics represent an ecological reserve function, providing biodiversity and regional heat island mitigation. This is a fundamentally different class of asset from ITU's 25 m²/person, which represents programmed, accessible amenity space.

4.0 Analysis of Development Intensity and Density


4.1 Building Density, Floor Area Ratio (FAR), and Building Coverage Ratio (BCR)

A primary finding of this analysis is the total absence of quantitative, official Floor Area Ratio (FAR/Emsal) and Building Coverage Ratio (BCR) values in the provided university planning documents.
This gap is itself a significant finding. While academic papers on Turkish urban planning regularly use these metrics (FAR, "bina yoğunluğu" or building density) 27, the university master plans provided appear to follow a different paradigm.
The METU master plan, for example, defines "Emsal" (FAR) and "Dolu-boş oranı" (Solid-Void Ratio / BCR) in its glossary, confirming their relevance.4 However, the plan's guiding principles are qualitative, focusing on "pedestrian-priority" and "spatial permeability" rather than assigning prescriptive numerical FAR/BCR limits to campus zones.4
This suggests that development is not governed by a rigid, quantitative zoning code. This conclusion is strongly supported by Bilkent University's "Quality of Life" strategic plan, which explicitly states: "Expansion/revision in the campus has to be based on a master plan, rather than an ad hoc approach".9 This admission of a historically "ad hoc" (project-by-project) development model explains the lack of public-facing, standardized density controls.
The only available proxy for building density is for METU. Using the ~450 building count 21 and the 800-hectare developed area from the 1961 plan 4, a low-density figure of ~0.56 buildings per hectare can be calculated.

4.2 Average Building Height

Specific data on building heights is not available. However, a reliable proxy for Koç University's average building height can be calculated by dividing its "Indoor Area" (GFA) by its "Building Footprint" area.
	•	Koç Avg. Height Proxy: 199,556 m² (GFA) 22 / 46,452 m² (Footprint) 5 = ~4.3 floors (average).
This figure is consistent with a modern, mid-rise campus design. No similar data was available for the other four universities.

5.0 Analysis of Campus Accessibility and Mobility


5.1 Road and Intersection Network

Quantitative data for road network length (km), road network density (km/km²), and intersection density (intersections/km²) are not available in the provided documents. The sources found pertaining to OpenStreetMap (OSM) discuss the methodology for conducting such network analyses 30 but do not contain the extracted data for these specific campuses.
The limited mobility data available is qualitative or relates to traffic volume:
	•	METU: The 1961 master plan's design is centered on a 1.5 km pedestrian spine, a key principle that separates vehicular and pedestrian traffic.4
	•	Bilkent: The campus functions as a major traffic generator. Its strategic plan notes that on a typical day, 10,000 vehicles enter the campus, and 5,000 use campus parking lots.9 The plan also identifies a significant planning challenge: campus roads are "increasingly used by transit traffic" as a shortcut 9, indicating a high and potentially problematic integration with the municipal road network.

5.2 Accessibility Compliance (TS 9111 & TS 12576)

The user query for a "Yes/No" compliance status for accessibility standards is not feasible. The data reveals a significant gap between legal requirements and physical reality, which is being managed procedurally.
The relevant, legally-mandated Turkish Standards are TS 9111 (Accessibility in buildings) 32 and TS 12576 (Structural Measures and Design Rules for Accessibility in Urban Roads/Pedestrian Areas).12 Public institutions are required to comply with these standards.12
However, field reports assessing Ankara university campuses conclude that physical infrastructure is non-compliant. One report states that "accessibility arrangements made are far from being in compliance with the standards" 12, and another notes that arrangements for orthopedic, visual, and hearing impairments are "deficient and far from TSI standards".12
The universities' response demonstrates this gap. Both Boğaziçi University and Koç University have established administrative bodies—a "Disability Commission" 11 and an "Office of Disability Services" 10, respectively. The stated goal of these offices is to "remove barriers" and "get the necessary arrangements of construction in the campus made".10 The existence of these offices confirms that barriers are present and that compliance is an ongoing process of mitigation, not a completed status.

6.0 Assessment of Seismic Resilience and Planning Compliance

Analysis of seismic compliance reveals a critical "seismic paradox": the universities whose faculty are the nation's foremost seismic experts are simultaneously owners of legacy buildings that do not meet current seismic codes.
The general context for Turkey is that seismic resilience is not a technical problem (the codes are state-of-the-art) but one of compliance, enforcement, and construction quality.26
Faculty from METU, Boğaziçi University, and ITU are the primary experts in this field. They served as coordinators and working group members for the 2003 "Earthquake Master Plan for Istanbul" 13 and are frequently cited as the leading authorities on seismic assessment.38
This expertise is precisely why these universities have identified their own structural risks. Both Boğaziçi University and ITU are participants in the World Bank-funded SREEPB (Seismic Resilience and Energy Efficiency in Public Buildings) Project.14 The project's explicit purpose is to "strengthen public buildings... that are at high seismic risk".14
Specific sub-projects are identified for structural strengthening and renovation at:
	•	Boğaziçi University Uçaksavar Campus: Targeting the Superdorm Student Dormitory.14
	•	Istanbul Technical University Ayazağa Campus: Targeting the Valley Dormitories Region.15
Therefore, the compliance status for BU and ITU is "In Progress." They have used their expertise to identify non-compliant, high-risk legacy structures and are actively mitigating them.
The status for the other universities is "Unknown." Koç University has an "Emergency and Disaster Management Center" and evacuation plans 25, which constitutes response planning rather than a structural compliance report. Bilkent University's architecture department has published guides on earthquake-resistant design in response to the 2023 earthquake, citing "non-compliance with seismic building codes" as a national problem 26, but no report on its own buildings' compliance is provided.

Figure 1: Comparative Benchmarking of Turkish University Campuses

A grouped bar chart plotting Total Campus Area (ha), Student Population, and Total Green/Forest Area (ha) for all five universities would visually demonstrate the findings of this report.
The chart would show that METU's bars for "Total Area" (4,500 ha) and "Green/Forest Area" (3,043 ha) are dramatically larger—by an order of magnitude—than those for any other institution. ITU would have the tallest bar for "Student Population" (38,636).
The other three universities (Boğaziçi, Koç, Bilkent) would appear clustered together at the low end of the land area and green area axes, despite being large institutions. This visualization provides an immediate and clear confirmation of the different campus typologies, distinguishing METU as a regional-scale land manager from the other four, which operate on a more conventional, building-centric campus scale.

Turkish Urban & Campus Planning: A Quantitative Framework for Computational Optimization


Executive Summary

Purpose: This report provides the definitive data, regulatory constraints, and cost models required for the development of PlanifyAI, a generative design and optimization system specialized for Turkish urban and campus planning.
Key Findings: The analysis is structured around four pillars, providing the foundational logic for the software's core modules:
	•	Taxonomy: A comprehensive database of Turkish building types (urban and campus) and their linkage to the official Sınıf (Class) system. This classification is the essential primary key that connects a building's function to its construction cost.
	•	Regulations: A quantitative breakdown of Turkish planning laws, including the İmar Kanunu (Zoning Law) and Planlı Alanlar İmar Yönetmeliği (Planned Areas Zoning Regulation). These laws are translated from legal text into algorithmic constraints (e.g., dynamic setbacks, FAR exclusions, parking ratios).
	•	Optimization: A multi-objective cost and accessibility model based on official 2025 Ministry construction cost data. This section defines the "fitness function" parameters for cost, adjacency, and environmental objectives.
	•	Strategy: A competitive analysis of incumbent tools (Autodesk Forma, TestFit) defining PlanifyAI's unique value proposition. The key differentiator is identified as "Automated Compliance & Feasibility for Turkey," a capability not offered by existing generic international software.
Critical Data Gaps (Action Required): The research successfully acquired core cost data 1, building classification summaries 2, and key zoning regulations.4 However, it failed to yield the specific quantitative planning standards from the Yükseköğretim Kurulu (YÖK) (Higher Education Council). These YÖK standards (e.g., m² per student for libraries, classrooms, and labs) are critical for the campus planning module's validity. The system cannot generate an accreditable university design without this data. Acquisition of YÖK's official Fiziksel Mekan Standartları (Physical Space Standards) is the highest-priority action item for the development of the campus planning module.

Part 1: The Turkish Building Taxonomy Database (The "Objects")

This part defines the "objects" (buildings) that the PlanifyAI system will generate, place, and analyze. It serves as the foundational dictionary, linking a building's function (e.g., "Library") to its official classification (e.g., "Class V-C"). This linkage is the essential mechanism for enabling automated cost estimation.

Section 1.1: Urban & Residential Building Taxonomy

This section details the common building types in a general urban or district-level plan, mapping them to the official construction classification system.
	•	Konut (Residential): This is not a single category. The system must differentiate functional types (e.g., Apartman, Müstakil Ev, Rezidans). The official classification is a starting point:
	•	Konutlar (dört kata kadar-dört kat dahil – asansörsüz, kalorifersiz) (Residences up to 4 floors, no elevator/heating) are classified as Class III-A.3
	•	This implies that taller, more complex apartment buildings or luxury Rezidans with elevators and central services would fall into higher, more costly classes (e.g., Class IV-A or IV-B).
	•	Muhtarlık (District Office):
	•	This is a minor civic structure. The Planlı Alanlar İmar Yönetmeliği (Planned Areas Zoning Regulation) draft specifies its characteristics when placed in parks: single story (max 4.50 meters height) and a small footprint (e.g., max 10 m²).4
	•	Based on its simplicity, it maps to Class I-A (Simple agricultural/basic structures) or Class II-B (Tek katlı ofisler - Single-story offices).3
	•	Sağlık Ocağı (Health Center):
	•	This is distinct from a full-service Hastane (Hospital), which is a complex Class V-B structure.2
	•	A local Aile Sağlığı Merkezi (Family Health Center) is a simpler public service building, permitted in parks over 5,000 m².4
	•	It maps most closely to Class III-B (similar to Kreş-Gündüz bakımevleri - Creches/Daycares) or Class IV-A.3
	•	Ticari (Commercial) & Alışveriş Merkezi (Malls):
	•	The regulations provide a clear hierarchy based on complexity 3:
	•	Tek katlı ofisler, dükkan ve basit atölyeler (Single-story offices, shops, simple workshops): Class II-B.
	•	Ticari bürolar (üç kata kadar...) (Commercial offices, up to 3 floors): Class III-A.
	•	Alışveriş merkezleri (Semt pazarları, küçük ve büyük hal binaları, marketler) (Shopping centers, district markets, wholesale markets, supermarkets): Class III-A.
	•	Mixed-Use (Karma Kullanım):
	•	The legal definitions include Ticaret+Konut (Commerce+Residential), Turizm+Ticaret (Tourism+Commerce), and Turizm+Ticaret+Konut.6
	•	Critical Constraint: The regulations state these areas "cannot be used as residential alone" (Tek başına konut olarak kullanılmamak koşuluyla).6 This is a vital Boolean constraint for the system's land-use assignment logic.
	•	Park ve Rekreasyon (Park and Recreation):
	•	The system must understand that parks are not empty land. Regulations allow for minor structures within park boundaries 4:
	•	Permitted Types: Büfe (Kiosk), Muhtarlık, Güvenlik Kulübesi (Security Hut), Sporcu Soyunma Kabinleri (Locker Rooms).
	•	Constraints: Max 1-story (4.50m height), max 10 m² footprint per structure. The total footprint of all such structures cannot exceed 3% of the park's total area.
	•	Larger Uses: Mescit (Small Mosque) and Aile Sağlığı Merkezi are permitted only in parks larger than 5,000 m².

Section 1.2: University Campus Building Taxonomy

This section defines the specialized buildings for the campus planning module, mapped to their official classifications. This mapping is essential for the campus cost-estimation function.
	•	Rektörlük (Rectorate):
	•	Function: Central administration building.
	•	Classification: Maps to İdari Binalar (Administrative Buildings). 3 lists İlçe tipi hükümet konakları (District-level government houses) as Class III-B. A large university rectorate, which is significantly more complex, would map to Class IV-B ("İdari Binalar," as listed in the full communiqué) or Class IV-C.
	•	Fakülte (Faculty) & Okul (School):
	•	Function: Primary education and research buildings, including classrooms, lecture halls, and offices.
	•	Classification: The regulations provide a clear hierarchy 2:
	•	Okul ve mahalle spor tesisleri (Basic schools and neighborhood sports facilities): Class III-A.
	•	Özelliği olan büyük okul yapıları (Spor salonu, konferans salonu ve ek tesisleri olan...) (Large school buildings with special features, e.g., gym, conference hall): Class IV-A.
	•	A typical university faculty building, with integrated labs, lecture halls, and specialized facilities, directly maps to Class IV-A.
	•	Lojman (Staff Housing):
	•	Function: Residential apartments for academic/administrative staff.
	•	Classification: Functionally identical to Konut (Residential). Maps to Class III-A 3 or a higher Class IV for taller, more complex structures with elevators.
	•	Kütüphane (Library):
	•	Function: Central and faculty-level libraries.
	•	Classification: 2 explicitly lists Müze ve kütüphane kompleksleri (Museum and library complexes) as Class V-C. This classification reflects their status as high-cost, specialized, large-span public buildings.
	•	Yemekhane (Cafeteria/Dining Hall):
	•	Function: Mass-scale food service.
	•	Classification: 3 explicitly lists Lokanta, kafeterya ve yemekhaneler (Restaurants, cafes, and dining halls) as Class III-B.
	•	Sosyal Tesis (Social Facilities):
	•	Function: A broad category including student clubs, event spaces, and congress centers.
	•	Classification: For large, specialized facilities, 2 lists Kongre merkezleri (Congress centers) as Class V-B. Simpler student club buildings would be classified lower, likely as Class III-B.
	•	Spor Salonu (Sports Hall):
	•	Function: Indoor and outdoor sports facilities.
	•	Classification: The regulations distinguish by scale 2:
	•	Okul ve mahalle spor tesisleri (School and neighborhood facilities): Class III-A. This represents the standard university gym.
	•	Olimpik spor tesisleri – hipodromlar (Olympic-level facilities, hippodromes): Class V-B. This would be a large campus stadium or arena.
	•	Laboratuvar (Lab) & Research Buildings:
	•	Function: Specialized R&D facilities.
	•	Classification: 2 lists Bilimsel araştırma merkezleri, AR-GE binaları (Scientific research centers, R&D buildings) as Class V-B. This applies to standalone, high-tech research centers. Basic teaching labs would be included within the Fakülte (Class IV-A) classification.

Section 1.3: Analysis: "Must-Have" vs. "Nice-to-Have" (10,000-Student University)

This section addresses Key Question 5 ("What are 'must-have' vs 'nice-to-have' buildings for a 10,000-student university?") by providing a programmatic model. This model can serve as the default "program" for the optimization system's campus module.
A "must-have" is any facility directly required for YÖK accreditation and basic student life. A "nice-to-have" supports quality of life, prestige, research expansion, or ancillary services.
	•	"Must-Have" Facilities (The MVP Campus):
	•	Fakülte Binaları (Faculty Buildings): The core of the university. Program based on student numbers and disciplines. (Class IV-A)
	•	Rektörlük/İdari Bina (Rectorate/Admin): Central administration. (Class III-B/IV-B)
	•	Merkezi Kütüphane (Central Library): Required for accreditation. (Class V-C)
	•	Merkezi Yemekhane (Central Dining Hall): Basic student services. (Class III-B)
	•	Temel Spor Tesisi (Basic Sports Hall): Basic student services/gym. (Class III-A)
	•	Laboratuvarlar (Laboratories): As required by discipline, either within Faculty buildings or as standalone R&D centers (Class V-B).
	•	"Nice-to-Have" Facilities (Expansion/Optimization Targets):
	•	Lojman (Staff Housing): (Class III-A)
	•	Yurt (Student Dormitory): (Likely Class III-B or IV-B)
	•	Sosyal Tesis / Kongre Merkezi (Social/Congress Center): (Class V-B)
	•	Olimpik Spor Tesisi (Olympic Sports Facility): (Class V-B)
	•	Çarşı (Market/Commercial Area): Small Ticari (Class II-B/III-A) units for on-campus services.
	•	Sağlık Merkezi (Health Center): (Class III-B/IV-A)

Section 1.4: The "Sınıf" (Class) as the Core Algorithmic Key

The development of a cost-aware optimization system is entirely dependent on linking a generated building (e.g., a "Faculty") to its correct price (e.g., TL/m²). Analysis of the Turkish regulatory and construction cost framework reveals a critical, non-obvious relationship:
	•	The optimization system generates functional buildings (e.g., Building_Type = Kütüphane).
	•	The official 2025 construction cost database 1 does not provide costs by function. It provides costs by an abstract classification system: Sınıf (Class) (e.g., Class V-C = 39.500,00 TL/m²).
	•	A separate set of architectural regulations 2 provides the mapping that links the function to the Sınıf (e.g., Müze ve kütüphane kompleksleri are defined as Class V-C).
Therefore, the Sınıf is the essential "primary key" or "join table" that connects the Taxonomy (Part 1) to the Cost Model (Part 3). The system's logic must execute this two-step lookup:

Section 1.5: OSM-to-Turkish Taxonomy Mapping Model

This section addresses Key Question 4 ("How to classify buildings from OSM data into Turkish building types?"). OpenStreetMap (OSM) data is user-generated and follows a global, generic tagging system 8 that does not align with the specific Turkish Sınıf system.
A direct 1:1 mapping is impossible. For example, amenity=cafe in OSM cannot be directly mapped; its classification and cost depend on its context. The PlanifyAI system must, therefore, employ a context-aware heuristic model to translate OSM data.
	•	Rule 1 (Landuse Context): The primary filter is the landuse tag (e.g., landuse=university, landuse=residential, landuse=commercial).
	•	Rule 2 (Tag Mapping): The meaning of a tag is interpreted through its context.
	•	amenity=cafe + landuse=university -> Turkish Type: Yemekhane -> Sınıf: III-B.
	•	amenity=cafe + landuse=commercial -> Turkish Type: Ticari (Dükkan) -> Sınıf: II-B or III-A.
	•	Rule 3 (Building Tag Priority): The building=* tag is more reliable for physical classification than amenity=*.8
	•	building=university + name="Mühendislik Fakültesi" -> Turkish Type: Fakülte -> Sınıf: IV-A.
	•	building=dormitory -> Turkish Type: Yurt -> Sınıf: III-B/IV-B.
	•	building=apartments -> Turkish Type: Konut (Apartman) -> Sınıf: III-A/IV-A.

Part 2: Planning Standards & Regulatory Constraints (The "Rules")

This part translates the Turkish legal planning framework into a set of quantitative constraints. These rules form the logical boundaries within which the generative design algorithm must operate.

Section 2.1: Core Zoning & Massing Regulations (İmar Kanunu & Yönetmeliği)

These regulations define the 3D "envelope" for any building.
	•	Emsal (Floor Area Ratio / KAKS) & Exclusions:
	•	This is the primary driver of development potential.
	•	A critical rule for the algorithm is the handling of Emsal Harici Alanlar (Areas excluded from FAR). The total area of these exclusions cannot exceed 30% of the total FAR-based area.4
	•	The generative model must include these "free" areas (which are still built and have a cost) without penalty to the legal FAR budget. These include 4:
	•	Otopark (Parking areas)
	•	Sığınak (Shelters)
	•	Yangın Merdivenleri (Fire Stairs)
	•	Asansör Boşlukları (Elevator Shafts)
	•	Isı Merkezi, Su Deposu (Heating Centers, Water Tanks)
	•	Mescit (Small Mosque, up to 100 m² in residences)
	•	An algorithm that only optimizes for sellable/usable area and fills 100% of the FAR with it will produce an illegal and unbuildable design. The 30% exclusion is a fundamental component of Turkish building massing.
	•	Setback Requirements (Çekme Mesafeleri):
	•	The regulations 4 provide static minimums (e.g., Minimum 3.00 meters for rear setbacks).
	•	More importantly, they introduce a dynamic constraint: setbacks are a function of building height.
	•	For buildings with more than 4 stories, the side and rear yard setbacks are increased by 0.50 meters for every floor above the fourth floor.4
	•	For "very high structures" (60.50 meters or more), the setback base becomes 15.00 meters. For every floor (calculated as 3.00m) above 60.50 meters, an additional 0.50 meters is added to this setback.4
	•	This is a critical non-linear relationship. The algorithm cannot set a fixed footprint (TAKS) and then extrude it. As the generative massing grows taller, the setback constraint simultaneously increases, which in turn shrinks the allowable footprint.
	•	Parcel Size & Frontage (Parsel Büyüklükleri):
	•	The regulations set minimum parcel sizes and frontages (widths) based on building type. These are hard constraints for land subdivision and validation.
	•	Example Constraint: Industrial zones and non-residential urban work areas require a minimum parcel frontage of 30.00 meters 4 and a minimum parcel area of 2000 m².6
	•	Example Constraint: Fuel stations require a minimum frontage of 40.00 meters.4
	•	Tree Planting Requirement:
	•	In the unbuilt garden setbacks of residential, education, health, and tourism parcels, one tree must be planted for every 15.00 m².4 This is a simple, quantifiable green space metric for the system to calculate and report.

Section 2.2: Spatial & Infrastructure Requirements

This section defines the rules governing the space between buildings.
	•	Walking Distance Standards (Answering Key Question 3):
	•	Hard Limit: 250 meters. This is the azami (maximum) walking distance specified in the Istanbul Parking Regulation for off-site parking solutions.11 This is a mandatory, legally-defined standard.
	•	Hard Limit: 45 meters. This is the maximum horizontal distance from the point of itfaiye (fire/emergency) vehicle access to any point on the building's exterior facade.5 This is a critical constraint for road layout and building placement.
	•	Soft Concept: The "15-minute city" (15 Dakikalık Şehir) 12 is a planning philosophy, not a legal standard in Turkey. It aims for 6 key functions (Housing, Work, Commerce, Health, Education, Entertainment) to be within a 15-minute walk (approx. 800m - 1.2km). This should be used as a scoring metric for the optimization's "accessibility" objective, not a hard constraint.
	•	Green Space Percentages (Kişi Başı):
	•	This is a significant data gap. The specific Mekansal Planlar Yapım Yönetmeliği (Spatial Plans Construction Regulation) detailing the kişi başına (per capita) green space and social infrastructure standards was not located.7
	•	The available data is conflicting, with academic sources citing ranges from 26 m²/person 13 to 14.09 m²/person.14
	•	Furthermore, there is a noted discrepancy between legal goals and on-the-ground reality. Studies of Turkish cities, including Istanbul, confirm that green space standards are often inadequately met 15 and that open greenery is limited.17
	•	Algorithmic Implication: The official standard (e.g., 10 or 15 m²/person, once acquired) must be an input parameter. The system should optimize for it, but it should be a soft constraint that can be reported as a "variance" rather than a hard failure, reflecting real-world development challenges.
	•	Parking Standards (Otopark Yönetmeliği):
	•	The parking calculation is a complex, multi-variable function. New regulations base the requirement not just on the number of units, but on the daire büyüklüğü (apartment size).18
	•	The system must also account for EV charging spaces, with new buildings requiring at least 5% of spots to be equipped for electric vehicles (rising to 10% for AVMs/malls).18
	•	The algorithm's parking calculation must be a function: Num_Spaces = f(Building_Type, Unit_Size_m²).

Section 2.3: University Campus Planning Standards (YÖK) (Answering Key Question 1)

This section addresses the requirements for the specialized campus planning module. This represents the most critical data gap in the current research. No quantitative standards from the Yükseköğretim Kurulu (YÖK) (Higher Education Council) were found in the available materials.
	•	Why YÖK Standards are Critical: Standard İmar Kanunu (Zoning Law) governs the building envelope. YÖK regulations govern the university's accreditation. YÖK dictates the minimum physical standards a university must provide for its students to be a functional, recognized institution.
	•	Required Data (To Be Acquired):
	•	Student-to-Facility Ratios: These are the core rules.
	•	Minimum Kütüphane (Library) m² per student.
	•	Minimum Yemekhane (Dining Hall) seats per student.
	•	Minimum Laboratuvar (Lab) m² per student (by discipline, e.g., Engineering vs. Arts).
	•	Minimum Derslik (Classroom) m² per student.
	•	YÖK Campus Planning Guidelines: YÖK likely has its own "planning guide" that outlines best practices for campus layout, internal road networks, accessibility, and green space, which may be stricter than the general law.
	•	Accessibility Requirements: Specific standards for disabled access, pedestrian networks, and on-campus transit.
	•	Algorithmic Implication: Without this YÖK data, PlanifyAI's campus module cannot generate a valid or accreditable university. It can only generate a collection of buildings. Acquiring YÖK's official Fiziksel Mekan Standartları (Physical Space Standards) is the highest priority for developing a defensible and functional campus planning product.

Part 3: Multi-Objective Optimization Parameters (The "Objectives")

This part defines the "fitness function" for the optimizer. A "good" plan is one that minimizes cost, maximizes accessibility, and meets environmental goals. This section provides the quantitative parameters for that function.

Section 3.1: Construction Cost Estimation Models (Answering Key Question 2)

	•	The Core Model: The total construction cost is a sum of the areas of all buildings multiplied by their specific unit costs.
	•	Total_Cost = Σ ( Area_of_Building_Type_X * Cost_of_Building_Type_X )
	•	The "Cost/m²" Formula (Lookup Logic):
	•	Cost_of_Building_Type_X = Cost_Lookup_Table_4
	•	Step 1: Take Building_Type (e.g., Hastane).
	•	Step 2: Use Table 1 to find its Sınıf (e.g., V-B).
	•	Step 3: Use Table 4 (below) to find the Cost/m² for that Sınıf (e.g., 35.600,00 TL/m²).
	•	Infrastructure Costs: The model must also add costs for land development, roads, and utilities. These are not included in the 1 data, which is per-building.19 This will require a separate cost model (e.g., cost per linear meter of road, cost per m² of excavation).

Section 3.2: Accessibility & Adjacency Metrics (Answering Key Question 6)

A "good layout" (Key Question 6) is one that is operationally efficient. It minimizes travel time between operationally-linked buildings and maximizes distance between conflicting uses. This can be modeled as a "force-directed" graph problem, where buildings exert "attraction" or "repulsion" forces on each other. The optimizer's goal is to find a layout that minimizes the total "energy" (conflict) of the system.
	•	High Attraction (+): Fakülte <-> Kütüphane (Students need to study), Fakülte <-> Yemekhane (Students need to eat), Lojman <-> Sosyal Tesis (Residents need amenities).
	•	High Repulsion (-): Lojman <-> Stadyum (Noise conflict), Araştırma (Lab) <-> Spor Salonu (Vibration/noise conflict), Rektörlük <-> Trafo Merkezi (Utility noise/aesthetics).
	•	Neutral (0): Rektörlük <-> Kütüphane.

Section 3.3: Environmental & Sustainability Constraints

The optimization function should include environmental objectives, which are increasingly codified in Turkish building standards.
	•	The Turkish Standard: B.E.S.T. The primary domestic green building certification in Turkey is B.E.S.T. (Binalarda Ekolojik ve Sürdürülebilir Tasarım), developed by the Turkish Green Building Council (ÇEDBİK).20
	•	Optimization Goals: The B.E.S.T. certification provides the categories for environmental scoring. For a layout planning tool, the most relevant categories are 23:
	•	Arazi Kullanımı (Land Use): Score layouts based on maximizing green space, protecting natural features, and minimizing impervious surfaces.
	•	Su Kullanımı (Water Use): Score layouts that facilitate rainwater harvesting and greywater recycling.
	•	Enerji Kullanımı (Energy Use): This is a key computational analysis. The system should score layouts based on:
	•	Solar Orientation: Maximizing southern exposure for Konut/Lojman (for passive heating) while minimizing E/W exposure for Fakülte/Ofis (to reduce cooling load).
	•	Wind Protection: Orienting buildings to block prevailing winter winds or channel summer breezes for passive cooling.
	•	Slope Limitations: As a standard geotechnical constraint, the system should penalize (i.e., add cost for excavation/foundations) building on slopes > 15% and prohibit building on slopes > 30% (or as specified by the user).

Part 4: Competitive Landscape & Strategic Differentiation

This part analyzes the market to define PlanifyAI's unique value proposition and defensible "moat."

Section 4.1: Analysis of Incumbent Tools (Forma & TestFit)

	•	Autodesk Forma:
	•	Strengths: Positioned as an "analysis" and "collaboration" powerhouse.24 Its core strengths are in evaluating existing designs, particularly for environmental factors like solar 26, wind, and carbon.25 Its integration with ArcGIS 27 provides rich GIS data context.
	•	Weaknesses: It is not a generative design tool for layout compliance. Its zoning/code compliance is handled via plug-ins 24, which are either generic (US-based) or require custom development by large firms. It does not contain the Turkish İmar Kanunu or Sınıf cost system out-of-the-box.
	•	TestFit:
	•	Strengths: A true generative tool focused on "AI site planning".29 It excels at rapid massing and real-time feasibility (calculating FAR, parking ratios, and yield on cost).30 It is a direct competitor to PlanifyAI's core function.
	•	Weaknesses: It is fundamentally US-centric. Its "configurators" are for US typologies.32 It has no concept of the Turkish Sınıf cost system, the İmar dynamic setback functions, or YÖK accreditation standards.

Section 4.2: Strategic Recommendations & Turkish Context

The competitive analysis reveals a clear path for PlanifyAI's market entry and differentiation.
	•	Go-to-Market Strategy:
	•	Do not compete with Forma on analysis. Forma is an ecosystem. PlanifyAI should be positioned as a tool that exports to Forma and Revit.
	•	Compete with TestFit on specificity. TestFit generates generic options. PlanifyAI must generate compliant and feasible options specifically for Turkey.
	•	The "Killer Feature": The value proposition is not "AI design." It is "Automated Compliance & Feasibility for Turkey." The system's ability to instantly generate a 3D massing that is guaranteed to be compliant with the complex İmar Kanunu (dynamic setbacks, 30% FAR exclusion, 45m fire access) and simultaneously pre-costed using the official 2025 Ministry Sınıf data 1 is a function that saves developers and architects weeks of manual feasibility work.
	•	The Niche Dominance: The YÖK Campus Planner is the high-value, defensible "moat." This is a specialized module for a high-value market (universities, public tenders, large architectural firms) that has zero existing automated solutions.
	•	The Turkish Context (Answering Key Question 6):
	•	Addressing "What makes a good campus layout according to Turkish planners?" requires understanding the conflict between aspiration and pragmatism in Turkish planning.
	•	Aspiration: Planners and academics value walkable, dense, mixed-use, and lively cities and campuses.17
	•	Pragmatism: The reality of Turkish urbanism is that cars are "omnipresent" 17, and green space goals are frequently unmet due to financial and development pressures.15
	•	Conclusion: A "good" plan in the Turkish context is one that is, first and foremost, financially viable and legally compliant. It maximizes the allowable Emsal (FAR) and solves the complex parking requirements.
	•	PlanifyAI's Strategic Role: The system should not merely enforce aspirational rules (like green space) that developers may be forced to ignore. Instead, it should be an optimization tool that proves these goals are not mutually exclusive. It can instantly demonstrate to a developer: "You can meet the 15 m²/person green space standard and still achieve 98% of your maximum profitable FAR if you use this optimized layout." It bridges the gap between aspiration and pragmatism by solving the complex 3D compliance and feasibility puzzle instantly.

A Technical Framework for Integrating Fast Urban Heat Island (UHI) Surrogates into Multi-Objective Spatial Planning Optimization


Part 1: The Physics and Dynamics of Urban Heat


1.1 Foundational Physics: The Urban Surface Energy Balance (SEB)

The foundation of all Urban Heat Island (UHI) modeling lies in the physics of the Surface Energy Balance (SEB), which dictates the thermal state of any surface. In a simplified, non-urban context, the SEB is a straightforward conservation of energy at an infinitely thin surface layer.1 The equation is:
$R_n = H + LE + G$
Where:
	•	$R_n$ is the net all-wave radiation (the net energy gained or lost via radiation).
	•	$H$ is the turbulent sensible heat flux (energy exchanged with the atmosphere via convection).
	•	$LE$ is the turbulent latent heat flux (energy consumed by evapotranspiration).
	•	$G$ is the ground heat flux (energy conducted into the substrate).
Urban environments fundamentally alter this balance. The "surface" is no longer a 2D plane but a 3D volume (the Urban Canopy Layer, or UCL), and human activity introduces a new, significant energy source.5 This requires an adaptation of the SEB into an Urban SEB (USEB), often expressed for the entire urban canopy volume.1 The canonical USEB equation is:
$Q^* + Q_F = Q_H + Q_E + \Delta Q_S$
Where the terms are defined as follows:
	•	$Q^*$ (Net All-Wave Radiation): This remains the primary energy input, representing the sum of net shortwave (solar) and net longwave (thermal) radiation: $Q^* = (K \downarrow - K \uparrow) + (L \downarrow - L \uparrow)$.7 In an urban canyon, this term is dominated by 3D morphology. Complex geometry (e.g., high aspect ratios) reduces $K \downarrow$ (direct solar gain) through shadowing but also traps $K \uparrow$ (reflected) and $L \uparrow$ (emitted) radiation, significantly altering the net balance.8
	•	$Q_F$ (Anthropogenic Heat Flux): This is the critical additive term in the urban equation.6 It represents the waste heat released from all human activities, primarily building HVAC systems, industrial processes, and vehicle combustion.11 In dense urban centers, $Q_F$ can exceed 100 $W/m^2$ 14 and is capable of raising local air temperatures by 1–3°C alone.11
	•	$Q_H$ (Turbulent Sensible Heat Flux): This is the convective heat exchange between the urban canopy (air, walls, roads) and the overlying atmosphere.1 $Q_H$ is the primary flux responsible for directly heating the urban air, contributing to the measurable UHI.7
	•	$Q_E$ (Turbulent Latent Heat Flux): This is the energy used for the phase change of water (evapotranspiration).1 In cities, the replacement of permeable, vegetated soils with impervious materials drastically reduces $Q_E$, converting what would be cooling (latent) heat into warming (sensible) heat.6
	•	$\Delta Q_S$ (Net Heat Storage): This term represents the net change in energy stored within the urban fabric (buildings, asphalt, concrete).1 Urban materials possess high thermal inertia (a function of density, specific heat, and thermal conductivity) 17, allowing them to absorb and store vast amounts of $Q^*$ and $Q_F$ during the day. This stored energy is then released at night, primarily as outgoing longwave radiation $L \uparrow$.
This storage-and-release dynamic governed by $\Delta Q_S$ is the primary driver of the nocturnal UHI phenomenon.20 While daytime UHI is complex, the pronounced warming observed at night is a direct consequence of the urban fabric slowly releasing its stored daytime heat, combined with the low Sky View Factor of canyons trapping this outgoing radiation.15 Any fast model intended for optimization must, therefore, use proxies that effectively capture this storage-and-release mechanism.

1.2 Key Contributing Drivers (Model Input Features)

To create a fast surrogate model, the complex physics of the USEB must be proxied by readily available GIS and geometric features. The following drivers are the most critical inputs for such a model.

3D Urban Morphology (SVF & H/W Ratio)

The most critical geometric parameter is the Sky View Factor (SVF), a dimensionless value from 0 (completely obstructed) to 1 (flat, open plain) that represents the fraction of the sky "seen" from a point.22 SVF governs the radiative exchange: it controls the amount of incoming solar radiation ($K \downarrow$) a surface can receive during the day and, critically, the amount of outgoing longwave radiation ($L \uparrow$) that can escape to the cold sink of space at night.9
The relationship between SVF and Land Surface Temperature (LST) is profoundly non-linear, making it a poor candidate for simple linear regression.
	•	Daytime (Shading Effect): A low SVF (e.g., a deep, narrow canyon) provides significant shading, blocking direct solar radiation and leading to daytime cooling compared to an open area.23
	•	Nighttime (Trapping Effect): The same low SVF geometry prevents radiative cooling by trapping outgoing $L \uparrow$, leading to significant nighttime warming.20
	•	Moderate SVF (Worst Case): Consequently, both very high SVF (open parks) and very low SVF (deep canyons) can be mitigative at different times. Studies, including one in Erzurum, Turkey 26, find that moderate SVF values—typical of mid-rise, medium-density developments—are often the worst-case scenario, aggravating the UHI.27
	•	Extreme SVF (Reversal): At extremely low SVF values (e.g., < 0.2), another reversal can occur where the shading benefit is overwhelmed by a lack of ventilation and the intense trapping of anthropogenic heat (AHF), leading to daytime warming.28
This complex, multi-modal behavior strongly supports the use of non-linear machine learning models (e.g., Random Forests, Neural Networks) as surrogates, as they can learn these interactions from data without requiring explicit physical formulation.

Material Properties (Albedo, Emissivity, Thermal Inertia)

The thermodynamic properties of the urban fabric control the $\Delta Q_S$ and $Q^*$ terms.
	•	Albedo: The solar reflectance of a surface.7 High-albedo materials (light-colored) form the basis of "cool roof" and "cool pavement" strategies.30 Concrete (albedo ~0.4) reflects significantly more solar radiation than asphalt (albedo ~0.1) 31, directly reducing the energy absorbed into $Q^*$.
	•	Emissivity: A material's efficiency at emitting thermal energy (heat).30 High-emissivity surfaces cool more effectively at night by radiating their stored heat.
	•	Thermal Inertia: The ability of a material to store heat.17 Urban materials like concrete and asphalt have high thermal inertia 19, driving the $\Delta Q_S$ term and contributing directly to the nocturnal UHI.21
A critical consideration for optimization is the albedo-comfort tradeoff. While an optimizer tasked with "minimizing LST" will favor high-albedo surfaces, this is not synonymous with improving human thermal comfort. High-albedo pavements can significantly reduce surface temperatures 33, but they do so by reflecting more solar radiation. This reflected energy is absorbed by pedestrians, increasing their mean radiant load and worsening thermal comfort indices like PET.35 A 2023 thesis from Middle East Technical University (METU) in Turkey confirmed this, finding that a "Cool Pavement" scenario was optimal for mitigating the UHI (air temperature), but a "Green" (vegetation) scenario was superior for improving perceived temperature (thermal comfort).36

Biogenic Factors (Green/Blue Infrastructure)

Vegetation is the most powerful UHI mitigation factor, primarily through two mechanisms: shading (reducing $Q^*$) and evapotranspiration (increasing $Q_E$).37
	•	Green Space Fraction (NDVI): The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) is a strong, consistent negative correlator with LST.38
	•	Impervious Surface Ratio (ISR): Conversely, the ISR is a strong positive correlator with LST.41
The cooling effect of vegetation is also non-linear. Research has identified a distinct performance threshold for tree canopy coverage. Cooling effects are minimal at low densities. However, once a local tree canopy coverage of approximately 40% is achieved, a step-change in cooling occurs, with air temperatures dropping by as much as 4–5°C.43 This provides a clear, actionable heuristic for spatial optimization: the algorithm should be incentivized to cluster vegetation to exceed this 40% local threshold, rather than "sprinkling" isolated trees.

Anthropogenic Heat Release (AHF)

For a 500x500m campus, AHF will be dominated by building energy consumption (HVAC) and local traffic.12 AHF can be estimated using "top-down" methods based on sectoral energy data 11 or "bottom-up" methods based on individual building energy simulations.46
For a fast optimization loop, simulating building energy for every iteration is computationally prohibitive. AHF must be proxied. The most effective GIS-based proxies are Building Density 11 and Total Gross Floor Area, which can be combined with a standard Energy Use Intensity (EUI) value ($W/m^2$) based on building typology (e.g., "university," "office") to create a robust AHF proxy feature.46

Part 2: A Decision Guide to UHI Modeling for Optimization


2.1 The Accuracy vs. Speed Trade-Off: The < 5-Second Constraint

The central challenge of this project is the conflict between model accuracy and computational speed. Multi-objective evolutionary algorithms (MOEAs) like NSGA-III require thousands, or tens of thousands, of fitness evaluations to converge on a Pareto-optimal front. The project constraint of < 5 seconds per evaluation 49 immediately dictates the feasible modeling approach.
	•	High-Fidelity Models (Non-Viable): Physics-based models like Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) and ENVI-met explicitly solve the Navier-Stokes equations for fluid flow and the full SEB.51 They are the "gold standard" for accuracy and are essential for post-optimization validation or offline training data generation.53 However, a single simulation for a 500x500m district can take minutes to many hours 55, making them computationally impossible for direct integration into an EA loop.
	•	Medium-Fidelity Models (Non-Viable): Radiation models such as SOLWEIG and RayMan are faster than full CFD.51 They excel at calculating specific parameters like Mean Radiant Temperature ($T_{mrt}$).53 However, their runtime is still in the minutes 55, far exceeding the 5-second constraint.
	•	Fast Heuristic & Surrogate Models (Viable Path): The only feasible approach is to use a model that provides a computationally trivial (millisecond-to-second) estimation. This can be a statistical model (e.g., GWR) or a machine learning (ML) surrogate.56 State-of-the-art research from 2024-2025 explicitly advocates for using ML models as "fast emulators" to replicate the behavior of complex physics models for exactly this purpose.57

Part 3: Formulating a Fast Surrogate Model for Fitness Evaluation


3.1 Approach 1: Statistical Models (MLR & GWR)

This approach establishes a direct statistical link between the GIS features of a layout and the target variable (LST), typically derived from satellite imagery.
	•	Mathematical Formulation (Multi-Linear Regression - MLR): The simplest model is a global MLR, which estimates a single set of coefficients for the entire study area.62 Based on key drivers 25, the equation takes the form: $LST = \beta_0 + \beta_1(SVF) + \beta_2(NDVI) + \beta_3(NDBI) + \beta_4(Albedo) + \beta_5(BuildingDensity) + \epsilon$ One study 60 derived the following coefficients for an OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) model: LST = 32.098 + 0.869(SVF) - 2.299(NDVI) + 11.969(NDBI) + 1.092(Albedo) +... This model is extremely fast to evaluate but suffers from a major limitation: it is a global model. The coefficients are static, but the underlying physics are not (e.g., the thermal effect of vegetation is different in a windy corridor vs. a sheltered courtyard). This is known as spatial non-stationarity.60
	•	Advanced Formulation (Geographically Weighted Regression - GWR): GWR, a local form of regression, solves this problem.59 Instead of one global equation, GWR fits a local MLR for every point (pixel) in the dataset, weighting the influence of neighboring points based on distance. The equation becomes: $LST(i) = \beta_0(i) + \sum_k \beta_k(i) \cdot X_k(i) + \epsilon(i)$ Here, the location (i) means that all coefficients $\beta_k(i)$ are functions of space and vary across the map. GWR has been shown to be highly effective for LST modeling. One study that compared OLS (MLR) to GWR for LST prediction found that GWR dramatically outperformed the global model, reducing the RMSE from 1.67 K to 0.26 K.60 This makes GWR a strong candidate for the fitness function, as it is spatially explicit and computationally fast enough for the task.

3.2 Approach 2: State-of-the-Art Machine Learning Emulators (Recommended)

This is the most robust and recommended approach, aligning with state-of-the-art research in 2024-2025.57 This method involves creating a "fast emulator" by training an ML model (e.g., Random Forest, XGBoost, or a Neural Network) to replicate the behavior of a high-fidelity physics model.
The optimal strategy is to generate a synthetic training dataset, as real-world satellite data is often noisy, incomplete due to clouds 64, and contains confounding variables (e.g., atmospheric conditions, antecedent weather) that are difficult to isolate.65
The synthetic training pipeline is as follows:
	•	Define Parameter Space: Identify the optimization variables (the "genes") that define a layout: e.g., building footprints, building heights, green space fraction, pavement albedo.
	•	Generate Scenarios: Synthetically generate a large (e.g., 1,000+) set of 500x500m campus layouts that randomly sample this parameter space.
	•	High-Fidelity Evaluation (Offline): Run a full ENVI-met or CFD simulation for all 1,000+ layouts. This is the computationally "expensive" step, which may take weeks of cluster computing but is only performed once.
	•	Create Training Data: For each simulated layout, extract (a) the input features (Avg. SVF, NDVI, Albedo, etc.) and (b) the target output (e.g., Peak LST, Avg. Air Temperature at 1.5m).
	•	Train Surrogate: Train a robust, non-linear ML model, such as XGBoost or Random Forest 66, on this (Features, LST) dataset. The resulting model file (e.g., uhi_surrogate.pkl) is a lightweight "emulator" that has learned the complex physics from the ENVI-met simulations.
This ML emulator is now custom-built for the specific site, climate (e.g., using a Turkish meteorological file), and design variables of the optimization problem. It can accurately predict the UHI impact of a new layout in milliseconds because it has internalized the complex, non-linear physics, such as the SVF and 40% canopy thresholds, from its training.

4.2 Python Implementation: LST Calculation from Landsat 8/9 (Validation)

To validate the surrogate model (Part 5.1), a ground-truth LST map must be derived from satellite imagery. The following is the standard mathematical workflow for calculating LST from Landsat 8/9 data.75
Step-by-Step Mathematical Formulation (Single-Band Method):
	•	Read Metadata (MTL file): Extract the following constants for Band 10:
	•	RADIANCE_MULT_BAND_10 (ML)
	•	RADIANCE_ADD_BAND_10 (AL)
	•	K1_CONSTANT_BAND_10 (K1)
	•	K2_CONSTANT_BAND_10 (K2)
	•	Convert DN to TOA Radiance ($L_{\lambda}$): The raw digital number ($Q_{cal}$) is converted to at-sensor spectral radiance.77 $L_{\lambda} = (ML \times Q_{cal}) + AL$
	•	Convert Radiance to Brightness Temperature (BT): Radiance is converted to at-satellite brightness temperature in Celsius using the thermal constants.75 $BT = \frac{K2}{\ln(\frac{K1}{L_{\lambda}} + 1)} - 273.15$
	•	Calculate NDVI: The vegetation index is calculated using Band 5 (NIR) and Band 4 (Red).75 $NDVI = \frac{(Band 5 - Band 4)}{(Band 5 + Band 4)}$
	•	Calculate Proportion of Vegetation ($P_v$): $P_v$ is calculated from the squared ratio of NDVI values.75 $P_v = \left( \frac{NDVI - NDVI_{min}}{NDVI_{max} - NDVI_{min}} \right)^2$
	•	Calculate Land Surface Emissivity ($\epsilon$): Emissivity is estimated based on the proportion of vegetation.75 $\epsilon = 0.004 \times P_v + 0.986$
	•	Calculate Final LST (Corrected for Emissivity): The brightness temperature is corrected using the calculated emissivity to find the true land surface temperature.75 $LST (^\circ C) = \frac{BT}{1 + \left( \frac{\lambda \times BT_{kelvin}}{c_2} \right) \times \ln(\epsilon)} - 273.15$
	•	Where $BT_{kelvin} = BT + 273.15$
	•	$\lambda$ = Wavelength of emitted radiance (10.895 $\mu m$ for Landsat 8 B10)
	•	$c_2 = 14388$ ($\mu m \cdot K$)
Conceptual Python Code (rasterio / numpy):

Python

4.3 Python Implementation: GWR Statistical Model (Alternative)

If the ML emulator approach (3.2) is not feasible due to the time required for synthetic data generation, the GWR model (3.1) is the recommended alternative. This can be implemented in Python using the pysal stack, specifically the mgwr library.81
The workflow would be:
	•	Load building and green space vectors (geopandas).
	•	Create a grid of points over the 500x500m campus.
	•	For each point, calculate the explanatory variables (SVF, NDBI, Albedo, Green Fraction) in a local buffer. This forms the X matrix.
	•	Load the Landsat-derived LST raster (from 4.2) and sample it at the grid points. This forms the dependent variable y.
	•	Use mgwr.sel_bw.Sel_BW(coords, y, X) to find the optimal kernel bandwidth.83
	•	Calibrate the model: gwr_results = mgwr.gwr.GWR(coords, y, X, gwr_bw).fit().85
	•	The gwr_results object can then be used to predict LST for new layouts by calculating their features (X_new) and applying the locally-weighted coefficients.

Part 5: Validation and Contextual Application


5.1 Validation and Calibration Protocol

The credibility of the surrogate model hinges on a rigorous validation protocol. A multi-step process is required:
	•	Benchmark Generation: A full, high-fidelity ENVI-met simulation is run for the baseline rowspan=""-existing 500x500m campus layout. This model is forced using typical Turkish summer meteorological data from ERA5 or TSMS.36
	•	Remote Sensing Validation: A cloud-free Landsat 8/9 image of the campus is processed using the LST workflow (4.2) to create a validation raster.75
	•	In-Situ Calibration: For maximum accuracy, the ENVI-met benchmark model should be calibrated. This involves deploying a small network of low-cost in-situ sensors (e.g., DS18B20 87) to collect ground-truth air temperature data, which is then used to fine-tune the ENVI-met model's boundary conditions.
	•	Surrogate Model Validation: The fast surrogate's LST prediction for the baseline layout is compared against both the ENVI-met LST output and the Landsat LST raster.
Error Metrics & Acceptance Criteria:
A realistic error bound must be established. State-of-the-art LST reconstruction models, which are themselves complex, report an RMSE of ~2.20 K and MAE of ~1.51 K against MODIS data.88 In complex urban cores, this error can rise to an RMSE of ~2.77 K.89 A surrogate model is not expected to be perfect; it is successful if it performs within the error bounds of these existing, accepted geophysical products.
Therefore, the acceptance criteria for the surrogate model are:
	•	RMSE < 3.0 K
	•	MAE < 2.0 K
	•	Pearson's R > 0.8 (to ensure strong positive correlation) 65

5.3 Application in the Turkish Context

The proposed framework is well-suited for the specified Turkish climate.
	•	Climate & UHI Magnitude: The context is hot summers (30–40°C) in cities like Istanbul (Mediterranean climate) and Ankara (Continental climate).96 The UHI effect is well-documented and significant in both cities. LST analysis confirms a strong UHI in Istanbul 98 and Ankara.102 Notably, Istanbul's UHI magnitude has been found to be at least 2.0°C larger than Ankara's.105
	•	Ankara UHI Anomaly: One study on Ankara found its UHI is strongest in winter.96 This is attributed to stable anticyclonic conditions and high AHF from domestic heating. While this project focuses on summer cooling, this finding reinforces the critical importance of including the Building Density (AHF proxy) feature in the surrogate model, as AHF is a dominant driver in the Turkish urban context.
	•	Relevant Case Studies: The 500x500m campus scale is well-supported by local research. Case studies on UHI effects have been conducted at several Turkish universities, including METU, Istanbul Technical University (ITU), and Bolu Abant Izzet Baysal University.36
	•	Regulatory Context: This optimization tool has direct policy applications. Turkish planning is governed by Zoning Law No. 3194.109 More recently, a national "Regulation on Green Certificate for Buildings" has been introduced.110 In Istanbul, the Metropolitan Municipality's (IMM) "Green City Action Plan" (GCAP) 111 and its goal to be carbon-neutral by 2050 111 are driving large-scale urban transformation.112 The Pareto-optimal solutions generated by this optimization framework can provide designers and planners with direct, evidence-based data to demonstrate how a proposed layout meets the performance goals of these new green regulations.

Part 6: Optimization and State-of-the-Art (2020-2025)


6.1 Integrating UHI with NSGA-III

The fast surrogate model (Part 3) serves as the 6th objective function for the MOEA. The optimization problem is formulated to minimize a vector of objectives:
$minimize F(x) = [f_1(x), f_2(x), f_3(x), f_4(x), f_5(x), f_6(x)]$
Where $f_6(x) = UHI\_surrogate(x)$ (Peak LST).
The NSGA-III (Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm III) is an excellent choice for this problem, as it is specifically designed to handle many-objective problems (i.e., more than 3) by using a reference-point-based selection mechanism, which maintains diversity along the Pareto-optimal front.113
The primary conflict the algorithm will navigate is the trade-off between development density (e.g., maximize Gross Floor Area, GFA) and UHI mitigation (e.g., minimize LST).116 A simple 2D analysis suggests these are in direct opposition.
However, a 3D-aware surrogate model allows the optimizer to "beat" this trade-off. Research shows that how density is arranged is more important than density itself. Specifically, "increasing building plan area causes a more rapid increase in UHI intensity than adding vertical building height".119 This implies that a layout with tall, slender buildings (low site coverage, high GFA) will have a lower UHI than a layout with low, sprawling buildings (high site coverage, same GFA). The tall/slender layout preserves precious ground-level green space for $Q_E$ (cooling) and maintains better airflow. The NSGA-III algorithm, guided by a 3D surrogate, will be able to discover these non-obvious "smart density" solutions that are optimal for both development and climate resilience, a finding supported by 2024 optimization studies.120

6.2 State-of-the-Art Review (2020-2025): UHI, MOEA, and ML

The proposed methodology is situated at the cutting edge of urban climate and computational design research.
	•	ML/CNN for UHI Prediction: The 2020-2025 period is defined by the widespread adoption of ML for UHI analysis.58
	•	A cornerstone 2025 paper (arXiv:2507.04802) provides direct validation for this report's core thesis.57 It explicitly proposes an "Interpretable Machine Learning" approach (Random Forest + XGBoost) as a "fast emulator" for a high-fidelity WRF-SLUCM physics model. The study confirms this method is accurate, computationally efficient, and allows for feature-importance ranking (identifying albedo, emissivity, and leaf area index as the most important drivers).
	•	Other recent work (2024) demonstrates the use of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to predict LST directly from Sentinel-2 and meteorological data, trained on high-resolution ECOSTRESS data.64 This image-to-image translation represents a next-generation approach.
	•	UHI in Multi-Objective Optimization (MOEA):
	•	Recent studies confirm the use of MOEAs for this exact problem. A 2024 study used NSGA-II coupled with the WRF-Chem model to find an "optimized spatial planning" solution for both UHI and air pollution, concluding that a "medium density and expanded urban green spaces" pattern is optimal.125
	•	The NSGA-III algorithm has been explicitly applied in 2024 for "Territorial Spatial Optimization... from the Perspective of Climate Resilience" 113 and for related sustainable planning problems like optimizing urban walkability.115
	•	Generative design studies, which use similar optimization loops, have coupled urban models with fast generators (like the Urban Weather Generator) to assess thousands of scenarios, concluding that "increasing greenery while reducing the site coverage ratio" is the most effective UHI mitigation strategy.126
This synthesis confirms that using NSGA-III to optimize a 3D layout, with a fast ML surrogate (trained on physics models) serving as the UHI fitness function, is precisely the state-of-the-art methodology as of 2024-2025.

Part 7: Concluding Recommendations


7.1 Final Model Recommendation for < 5-Second Evaluation

Based on the constraints of the optimization task and the state-of-the-art, the primary recommendation is the Machine Learning (XGBoost) Surrogate Model.
This model must be:
	•	Trained Offline: Using a large (1,000+) synthetic dataset of 500x500m campus layouts.
	•	Based on Physics: The training data's target (LST) must be generated by a high-fidelity physics model (e.g., ENVI-met) that is parameterized for the Turkish summer climate (using ERA5 or TSMS data).
	•	Feature-Driven: The model's inputs must be fast-to-calculate GIS/geometric proxies for the core physics: Average SVF, Green Space Fraction, Impervious Fraction, Area-Weighted Average Albedo, Building Density (as an AHF proxy), and Average H/W Ratio.
This approach is computationally feasible (prediction time < 1 second), aligns with 2025 research 57, and is uniquely capable of learning the non-linear physical interactions (e.g., the 40% canopy threshold 43 and complex SVF dynamics 27) that a simple linear model would fail to capture.

7.2 Limitations and Avenues for Future Research

This framework, while robust, has specific limitations that inform future research directions.
	•	LST vs. Thermal Comfort: The primary limitation is the objective function. As identified in studies 35, minimizing LST is not synonymous with maximizing human thermal comfort (e.g., PET). High-albedo solutions, which the optimizer will favor, can worsen comfort by reflecting solar radiation onto pedestrians. Future work should train the surrogate to predict a comfort index like PET, not just LST, to resolve this conflict.
	•	Model Generalizability: The recommended surrogate is hyper-specific. It is trained for a 500x500m campus in a Turkish climate. It is not generalizable and cannot be applied to other cities or scales without being completely retrained on a new synthetic dataset.
	•	Static AHF: The AHF proxy (based on building density) is static. It does not create a feedback loop. A more advanced framework would dynamically couple the UHI surrogate with a Building Energy Model (BEM) surrogate.61 In this "co-simulation," as the optimizer changes a building's form, its HVAC energy (and thus its AHF) would also change, creating a true feedback loop. This, however, significantly increases complexity and may violate the < 5-second computational constraint.

A Technical Guide to Flood Risk Assessment and Stormwater Management Optimization for Urban Spatial Planning


Part 1: Principles of Hydrological Modeling for Urban Catchments

The quantification of stormwater runoff is the foundation of flood risk assessment. This process involves modeling the transformation of rainfall into overland flow, determining both the total volume of water and its peak discharge rate. The selection of a model depends on the required output (peak flow vs. volume) and the allowable computational expense.

1.1 Rainfall-Runoff Generation: Formulations and Application


1.1.1 The Rational Method (Q=CIA): Peak Flow Estimation

The Rational Method is an empirical formula used for estimating the peak discharge from small drainage areas, typically less than 20 acres, though some jurisdictions permit up to 160 acres.1 It is not suitable for complex topography or for calculating runoff volume.1
The method is defined by the equation 1:
$$Q = C \cdot i \cdot A$$
Where:
	•	$Q$ = Peak flow rate (e.g., m³/s or ft³/s).
	•	$C$ = Runoff coefficient (dimensionless). This is the key variable for spatial planning, representing the integrated hydrological response of a surface.
	•	$i$ = Rainfall intensity (e.g., mm/hr or in/hr) for a specific storm frequency (e.g., 100-year) and a duration equal to the Time of Concentration ($T_c$).
	•	$A$ = Drainage area (e.g., hectares or acres).
The runoff coefficient $C$ aggregates complex processes like infiltration and surface storage into a single value representing the ratio of runoff to rainfall.5 It is highly dependent on land cover, as shown in Table 1.1.

The Time of Concentration ($T_c$) is the time required for water to flow from the hydraulically most distant point in the watershed to the outlet.2 This is a critical parameter, as the rainfall intensity $i$ is selected from an IDF curve based on this duration.
For integration with a spatial optimization algorithm, a static $C$ value is insufficient. Instead, a composite $C$ value must be dynamically calculated for each proposed layout $x$:
$$C_{\text{composite}}(x) = \frac{\sum (A_i \cdot C_i)}{A_{\text{total}}}$$
This $C_{\text{composite}}$ provides the direct, high-speed link between a proposed spatial plan (the areas $A_i$ of buildings, grass, and Green Infrastructure) and its peak hydrological response ($Q$).

1.1.2 The SCS/NRCS Curve Number (CN) Method: Runoff Volume Estimation

The SCS/NRCS Curve Number (CN) method is a more sophisticated empirical model used to estimate the volume of direct runoff (precipitation excess, $P_e$) from a given rainfall event ($P$).6 It is responsive to soil type, land use/cover, and antecedent soil moisture.7 This method is essential for designing Green Infrastructure (GI) intended to meet specific retention volume requirements.
The methodology is sequential:
	•	Potential Maximum Retention ($S$): First, the $CN$ (a value from 30 to 100) is determined for the site based on land use and hydrologic soil group. The maximum retention $S$ is then calculated from the $CN$ 8:
	•	(Metric) $S = \frac{25400}{CN} - 254$ (in mm)
	•	(Imperial) $S = \frac{1000}{CN} - 10$ (in inches)
	•	Initial Abstraction ($I_a$): This represents all initial losses before runoff begins (e.g., interception, depression storage). It is empirically related to $S$ 8:
	•	$I_a = 0.2 \cdot S$
	•	Precipitation Excess ($P_e$) / Runoff Volume: The runoff volume $P_e$ is calculated based on the total storm precipitation $P$. Runoff only occurs if $P > I_a$.6
	•	If $P \le I_a$: $P_e = 0$
	•	If $P > I_a$: $P_e = \frac{(P - I_a)^2}{P - I_a + S}$ or (substituting $I_a = 0.2S$): $P_e = \frac{(P - 0.2S)^2}{P + 0.8S}$
This method is critical for formulating regulatory constraints. For example, many regulations (including the intent of the 2017 Turkish regulation 9) require capturing a "first flush" volume, often standardized as the runoff from a 25mm storm.10 To implement this as an optimization constraint, the required retention volume is calculated by setting $P = 25$ mm and using the composite, post-development $CN$ to find $S$. The resulting $P_e$ (multiplied by the site area) becomes the minimum volume the proposed GI network must be designed to store.

1.1.3 Dynamic Modeling: EPA Storm Water Management Model (SWMM)

The EPA Storm Water Management Model (SWMM) is a comprehensive, dynamic rainfall-runoff simulation model.11 It is used for both single-event and long-term continuous simulation of runoff quantity and quality, primarily in urban areas.11
	•	Hydrology: SWMM models each subcatchment as a nonlinear reservoir. It solves for flow depth using Manning's equation and routes flow based on surface slope and width.
	•	Hydraulics: It transports this runoff through a user-defined network of pipes, channels, storage units, and regulators.11 It can solve the full 1D Saint-Venant equations (Dynamic Wave routing), providing a highly accurate simulation of channel flow, backwater effects, and looped connections.13
For the purposes of optimization, SWMM's high fidelity is its primary drawback. A single simulation can take several minutes 14, making it computationally prohibitive for use as a fitness function within an Evolutionary Algorithm (EA) that requires thousands of evaluations. Therefore, SWMM's role is not optimization but validation. It serves as the "ground-truth" model to (a) validate the accuracy of the fast proxy model and (b) validate the final optimized design produced by the EA.15 The pyswmm Python library facilitates this by allowing scripted interaction with the SWMM engine.16

1.2 Digital Terrain Analysis and Flow Routing

This component models where the generated runoff will move across the site's topography. This is achieved through raster-based analysis of a Digital Elevation Model (DEM).

1.2.1 Flow Direction Algorithms: D8 vs. D-Infinity (DINF) vs. MFD

The algorithm used to determine flow direction from a DEM grid cell fundamentally impacts the resulting hydrological pathways.
	•	D8 (Deterministic 8): This is the most common and computationally simplest method. Flow from the center cell is assigned entirely to one of its eight adjacent or diagonal neighbors: the one with the steepest downslope gradient.18 While fast, D8 is a non-dispersive algorithm.20 This tends to produce unrealistic, parallel flow paths on planar or convex hillslopes and is best suited for well-defined, convergent channels.20
	•	Multiple Flow Direction (MFD): This algorithm, described by Qin et al. (2007), partitions flow from one cell to all of its downslope neighbors.18 The flow is proportioned based on the slope to each neighbor. This is a highly dispersive model, effective for representing overland sheet flow but less so for channelization.21
	•	D-Infinity (DINF): Developed by Tarboton (1997), DINF determines flow direction as a single, continuous angle (a floating-point number) representing the steepest slope on eight triangular facets created in a 3x3 window.18 This allows flow to be proportioned between two downslope cells, providing a more realistic representation of flow dispersion on hillslopes than D8 without the excessive dispersion of MFD.22
The choice of algorithm is purpose-driven. For delineating channel networks, D8 is often sufficient. However, for the primary goal of this project—creating a fast inundation proxy—the Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) method is used. HAND is fundamentally a calculation of vertical hillslope distance to the nearest channel.23 Therefore, the D-Infinity (DINF) algorithm is the correct choice for this application, as it more accurately models flow paths across hillslopes.22

1.2.2 Flow Accumulation Algorithm (D8 Pseudocode)

Flow accumulation calculates the total number of upslope cells draining into each cell, identifying areas of concentrated flow (i.e., streams). It requires a flow direction grid as input. While many algorithms exist, a stack-based (non-recursive) approach is highly efficient and common in modern GIS packages.24
The following pseudocode outlines the logic for a D8 flow accumulation:

Kod snippet'i


function D8_Flow_Accumulation(flow_direction_grid, dem):   // 1. Initialization   rows, cols = dimensions(dem)   accumulation_grid = initialize_grid(rows, cols, value=1)   in_degree_grid = initialize_grid(rows, cols, value=0)   processing_stack = new Stack()    // 2. Calculate in-degrees (how many cells flow INTO this cell)   for r from 0 to rows:     for c from 0 to cols:       (next_r, next_c) = get_downslope_neighbor(flow_direction_grid, r, c)       if (next_r, next_c) is a valid cell:         in_degree_grid[next_r][next_c] += 1    // 3. Identify headwater cells (in-degree is 0) and push to stack   for r from 0 to rows:     for c from 0 to cols:       if in_degree_grid[r][c] == 0:         push (r, c) onto processing_stack    // 4. Process the stack in topological order   while processing_stack is not empty:     (r, c) = pop from processing_stack          // 5. Find the single downslope neighbor (D8 logic)     (next_r, next_c) = get_downslope_neighbor(flow_direction_grid, r, c)          if (next_r, next_c) is a valid cell:       // 6. Pass accumulation value       accumulation_grid[next_r][next_c] += accumulation_grid[r][c]              // 7. Reduce in-degree of the neighbor       in_degree_grid[next_r][next_c] -= 1              // 8. If neighbor has no more upslope cells to process, add to stack       if in_degree_grid[next_r][next_c] == 0:         push (next_r, next_c) onto processing_stack            return accumulation_grid 
This logic is implemented in Python libraries like pysheds 25 or can be built using rasterio 26 and scipy.ndimage.27

Part 2: Quantifying Flood Risk and Vulnerability

Quantifying risk requires translating the hydrological "source" (runoff) into a "threat" (inundation) and assessing its impact on "receptors" (buildings and infrastructure).

2.1 Design Storm Formulation: From Return Periods to Hyetographs

	•	Return Period (T): A 100-year flood is a flow event that has a 1% chance of being equaled or exceeded in any given year, known as the 1% Annual Exceedance Probability (AEP).28 The choice of return period (e.g., 10-year, 50-year, 100-year) depends on the design standard and the criticality of the infrastructure.29
	•	Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) Curves: This is the standard tool for defining the design storm.30 An IDF curve is a set of relationships, typically empirical equations, that define the average rainfall intensity $i$ for a given duration $d$ and return period $T$.30
	•	Using the IDF Curve:
	•	Determine Duration ($d$): For the Rational Method, the duration is set equal to the site's Time of Concentration ($T_c$).2
	•	Find Intensity ($i$): Using the 100-year IDF curve for the project location (e.g., Istanbul), the intensity $i$ corresponding to duration $d$ is identified. This $i$ is used directly in $Q=CiA$.
	•	Develop Hyetograph: For dynamic models (SWMM/HEC-RAS), a full storm hydrograph (a time-series of rainfall) is needed, not just a peak intensity. This hyetograph is also derived from the IDF curve, using a synthetic storm distribution like the Alternating Block Method.32

2.2 Flood Inundation Mapping Methodologies


2.2.1 High-Fidelity: 2D Shallow Water Equations (SWE)

The most accurate method for urban inundation mapping is to solve the two-dimensional (2D) Shallow Water Equations (SWE), which govern the conservation of mass and momentum.33 Models like HEC-RAS 2D 34 and MIKE 21 33 implement this. They can accurately simulate complex hydrodynamic behaviors critical in urban areas, such as inertial effects, backwater from obstructions, and flow velocities.34
The primary drawback is computational expense. A high-resolution 2D HEC-RAS simulation can require 24 to 45 hours to run.14 This high "computational overhead" 34 makes 2D SWE models completely unsuitable for an EA fitness function, which must be evaluated thousands of times. Their role is restricted to validation of the final, optimized design.

2.2.2 Simplified Models (The "Fast" Alternatives)

To meet the <5 second evaluation constraint, a simplified proxy model is required.
	•	Kinematic Wave: This is a simplification of the SWE that neglects pressure, velocity, and inertial terms.33 It assumes the water surface slope equals the bed slope.38 It is much faster but loses accuracy in flat terrain or where backwater effects are significant.
	•	GIS "Bathtub" Model: The simplest approach. A total flood volume (from SCS-CN) is "poured" onto the DEM. All cells below the resulting water surface elevation are marked as "flooded." This model is very fast but hydraulically incorrect, as it ignores flow paths and conveyance.
	•	Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND): This is the selected proxy model. HAND is a terrain-based proxy that relates topography to flood stage.39 For every cell on the landscape, HAND is its vertical elevation difference relative to the nearest cell identified as part of the drainage network.23 An inundation map is then generated by thresholding the HAND raster: all cells where $HAND < \text{Flood Stage}$ are considered inundated.40 This is a simple, hydrologically-informed raster operation that runs in milliseconds, making it the ideal core for the fast fitness function.

2.3 Vulnerability and Damage Assessment

Flood risk is the product of the hazard, the elements exposed to it, and their vulnerability.41
	•	Hazard Quantification: The output of the flood model, defined by:
	•	Flood Depth: The primary driver of building damage.41
	•	Flood Velocity: Critical for structural integrity and human safety.42
	•	Hazard Index: These factors are often combined into a single hazard index. A common formula is 43: $Hazard = \text{Depth} \times (\text{Velocity} + 0.5)$ A value $> 2.5$ is considered "Extreme" danger, while $< 0.75$ is "Low".43
	•	Exposure: The elements at risk. In this context, this is the output of the optimizer's building placement algorithm.
	•	Vulnerability: The susceptibility of the exposed elements. This is quantified using depth-damage curves, which relate water depth to a monetary damage cost for a specific building type (e.g., masonry, single-story).42 Vulnerability can be reduced through adaptive capacity, such as elevating foundations or using flood-resistant materials.45
The fitness function $f_1(\text{risk})$ can thus be formulated with increasing complexity:
	•	Simple (Fastest): $f_1 = \text{minimize(Total Inundated Area)}$
	•	Better (Chosen): $f_1 = \text{minimize(Sum of Inundation Depth at Building Footprints)}$
	•	Best (Slowest): $f_1 = \text{minimize(Sum of Expected Damage Cost)}$ (requires lookup of depth-damage curves for each building).
For the <5s constraint, the second formulation (sum of depth at buildings) provides the best balance of speed and accuracy.

Part 3: Fast Computational Models for Evolutionary Algorithm Integration (The <5s Evaluation)

Meeting the <5 second evaluation time constraint is the central technical challenge. This requires moving from high-fidelity simulation to high-speed proxy modeling. The computational workflow must be pre-calculated, leaving only minimal, near-instantaneous calculations inside the optimization loop.

3.1 GIS-Based Rapid Assessment: The Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) Method

The HAND method is the primary recommended approach. It is a terrain-based proxy that pre-calculates the relative elevation of the entire landscape, allowing for "on-the-fly" inundation mapping based on a single flood stage value.39
Computational Workflow (Pre-processing):
The following steps are performed once before the optimization begins, using a high-resolution DEM (e.g., 5m).
	•	Input: High-resolution DEM (e.g., from Copernicus or local LiDAR).26
	•	Step 1: DEM Conditioning: Sinks and depressions in the DEM are filled to ensure continuous flow paths. This is done using libraries like pysheds (e.g., grid.fill_depressions).22
	•	Step 2: Flow Direction: A D-Infinity (DINF) flow direction grid is computed.22 This is critical for accurately modeling the hillslope paths that define HAND.23 (e.g., pysheds: grid.flowdir(routing='dinf')).
	•	Step 3: Flow Accumulation: A DINF flow accumulation grid is generated.22
	•	Step 4: Define Drainage Network: The accumulation grid is thresholded to create a raster mask of the stream/drainage network (e.g., drainage_mask = acc_grid > 1000). This threshold defines what the model considers a "drainage" channel.47
	•	Step 5: Calculate HAND: The vertical distance from every cell to the nearest cell in the drainage_mask is calculated. pysheds provides a direct function: grid.compute_hand().48
The result is a hand.tif raster where each cell's value is its elevation (in meters) above the nearest stream.
Using HAND as a Fitness Function (Inside the <5s Loop):
This pre-computed HAND raster allows for an extremely fast fitness evaluation:
	•	The EA proposes a layout (building and GI locations).
	•	The layout's total impervious surface ratio is used in a simplified model (e.g., Rational Method, Part 1.1.1) to estimate a peak discharge (Q).
	•	This $Q$ is converted to a flood stage (H) (e.g., 2.5m) using a pre-computed synthetic rating curve.39
	•	Fitness Evaluation (milliseconds): The flood risk is calculated using fast raster algebra (e.g., with numpy):
	•	inundation_depth = np.maximum(0, H - hand_raster)
	•	depth_at_buildings = inundation_depth * layout_buildings (where layout_buildings is a binary array from the EA)
	•	f1_risk = np.sum(depth_at_buildings)
This workflow is exceptionally fast. The expensive GIS operations (flow routing, HAND calculation) are done once. The iterative fitness evaluation is just array arithmetic. Low-complexity models like this are recognized as suitable, rapid alternatives to HEC-RAS 2D for fast predictions in time-limited scenarios.34

3.2 Surrogate Modeling: Machine Learning for Runoff Prediction (Advanced Alternative)

An alternative is to create a surrogate model (or metamodel) that emulates the complex behavior of a high-fidelity model like SWMM.51
Methodology:
	•	Offline Training: This is a computationally expensive pre-processing step. The high-fidelity SWMM/HEC-RAS model is run thousands of times with varying inputs (e.g., different building layouts, GI placements).
	•	Training Data: The inputs (layout features) and outputs (flood risk metrics) are collected:
	•	Inputs ($X$): $$ * Outputs ($Y$):$$
	•	Model: A machine learning model, such as a Neural Network 53, Support Vector Regression 54, or Random Forest 55, is trained to learn the function $Y = f(X)$.
State-of-the-Art (CNNs): Recent research (2020-2025) uses Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for this purpose, as they can interpret spatial data.56 A U-Net architecture, for example, can take a multi-channel image (DEM, land use, rainfall) as input and output a 2D image (raster) of the predicted flood depths.58
Using as Fitness Function (Milliseconds):
Once trained, the surrogate model is extremely fast. The fitness evaluation becomes a single prediction call:
f1_risk = trained_cnn_model.predict(layout_as_raster).60
This approach requires a massive upfront training investment but provides a highly accurate, near-instantaneous fitness function. The HAND method is generally preferred for novel-site deployment due to its lower setup cost.

Part 4: Stormwater Management Strategies: GI, LID, and Blue-Green Infrastructure

These strategies form the "toolkit" for the optimization algorithm—the set of solutions it can place on the site to mitigate flood risk and meet regulatory constraints.

4.1 Green Infrastructure (GI) and Low Impact Development (LID)

The philosophy of GI/LID is to manage stormwater at its source by mimicking natural hydrological processes: infiltration, evapotranspiration, and detention.61
	•	4.1.1 Infiltration-Based:
	•	Bioswales / Vegetated Swales: Shallow, vegetated channels that slow stormwater, filter pollutants, and promote infiltration, rather than just conveying it.61
	•	Rain Gardens / Bioretention: Engineered depressions containing specific soil media, mulch, and vegetation. They are designed to capture, store, and infiltrate runoff from nearby impervious surfaces.63
	•	Permeable Pavements: Includes porous asphalt, pervious concrete, and permeable interlocking pavers. These surfaces allow rainwater to infiltrate into a sub-base storage layer, reducing runoff volume and filtering pollutants.61 They require maintenance to prevent clogging.65
	•	4.1.2 Storage/Evapotranspiration-Based:
	•	Green Roofs: A roofing system composed of vegetation and a growing medium planted over a waterproofing membrane. They absorb and evapotranspire significant amounts of rainfall, delaying and reducing runoff peaks and volumes.61 A field study in Istanbul, for example, found an average retention of 80.8% and significantly reduced runoff curve numbers (CN 84-90) compared to a conventional roof (CN 98).67

4.2 Blue-Green Infrastructure (BGI)

BGI combines the ecological benefits of green infrastructure with the storage and conveyance functions of "blue" infrastructure (water bodies).
	•	Retention Ponds (Wet Ponds): Constructed basins that maintain a permanent pool of water.68 They manage stormwater by detaining runoff and settling pollutants.69
	•	Constructed Wetlands: A more advanced form of retention pond that incorporates wetland vegetation. They are highly effective at pollutant removal and attenuating flood flows by mimicking natural wetland functions.69

4.3 Grey Infrastructure Integration

GI/LID does not typically replace traditional "grey" infrastructure (pipes and drains) but rather complements it. GI is used to reduce the peak load and volume entering the grey system.69
	•	Detention Basins (Dry Ponds): These are basins designed to temporarily store stormwater runoff and release it at a controlled rate through an outlet structure. They are dry between storm events.69
	•	Storm Drains: The conventional network of pipes. GI reduces the required diameter and capacity of this network, often leading to cost savings.75

4.4 Quantifying GI Effectiveness

For the optimizer's cost-benefit analysis, each GI/LID practice must be assigned a quantitative effectiveness value. This data is synthesized from modeling studies and technical manuals.
A key validation benchmark comes from a 2020 study on the highly urbanized Ayamama watershed in Istanbul. Using SWMM, it was found that combining green roofs and permeable pavements achieved a 56.02% peak runoff reduction and a 58.79% volume reduction for a 10-year return period storm.32 This provides a specific, localized target for optimization performance.

Part 5: Case Study: The Turkish Context

Grounding the model in the specific environmental and regulatory context of Turkey is essential for its practical application and validation.

5.1 Vulnerability Analysis


5.1.1 Istanbul: Ayamama and Alibeyköy Basins

Istanbul is exceptionally vulnerable to flash floods, a risk exacerbated by rapid, uncontrolled urbanization, particularly in sensitive basins.77
The Ayamama Watershed is a critical case study. The catastrophic 2009 flood, which resulted in 32 fatalities, was directly attributed to an "especially high number of constructions on the floodplain".80 Hydrodynamic modeling of this event using SWMM confirmed that flood depths reached approximately 11 meters in critical regions, with a flood extent of 1,200 meters.80 This 2009 event provides a stark, real-world validation target for high-fidelity models. Furthermore, modeling studies using SLEUTH (a cellular automata urbanization model) confirmed that continued development in the watershed will considerably increase the inundated area.36
This specific watershed provides two crucial validation points:
	•	Extreme Event Validation: A calibrated high-fidelity model must be able to replicate the 11m depths of the 2009 flood.80
	•	Mitigation Validation: The optimization solution should be benchmarked against the 2020 SWMM study on the same watershed, which demonstrated that a combination of green roofs and permeable pavements could achieve a 56.02% peak reduction for a 10-year storm.32

5.1.2 Ankara: Infrastructure and "Sponge City" Goals

Ankara faces significant water security and climate adaptation challenges.82 In response, the Ankara Green City Action Plan (GCAP) has been developed, with a stated strategic goal of transforming Ankara into a "sponge city".83 This policy explicitly calls for the implementation of Green Infrastructure (GI) 84 and green roofs 85 to manage stormwater and enhance resilience. The optimization framework detailed in this report provides a direct, quantitative tool to achieve this "sponge city" objective, moving it from a policy goal to an engineered reality.

5.2 Regulatory and Climatic Framework


5.2.1 Turkish Stormwater Regulations (ISKI)

The primary national policy is the "Regulation on stormwater collection, storage, and discharge systems" (2017).9 This regulation marks a shift toward modern stormwater management. Its key principles explicitly promote groundwater recharge and rooftop rainwater harvesting.9 This provides a clear legal mandate for the use of LID and GI techniques.
While the regulation provides technical details on pipe sizing (e.g., max 90% full) and flow velocity (0.5-5 m/s) 9, its intent is to promote infiltration. To translate this intent into a hard, quantifiable constraint for the optimization, a "first flush" capture requirement is appropriate. A common best-practice standard is the capture and management of the first 25mm of rainfall, which typically carries the highest pollutant load.10 This 25mm retention volume, calculated via the SCS-CN method (Part 1.1.2), will serve as the primary regulatory constraint ($g_1(x)$) for the optimizer.

5.2.2 Climate Change Projections (CMIP6 for Turkey)

Designing for flood resilience based only on historical rainfall data is no longer scientifically defensible. Recent climate projections for Turkey, using the latest CMIP6 models (Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6), indicate a clear and concerning trend. While total annual precipitation may decrease in some regions, there will be a significant increase in the frequency and intensity of extreme rainfall events.87 Projections show a shift toward "shorter-duration but higher-intensity rainfalls".87
This non-stationarity of rainfall means that the historical 100-year IDF curve under-represents future risk. This creates a "Safe Development Paradox" 89, where infrastructure designed to be "safe" for a 100-year event may fail catastrophically, inducing more development in a risky area.
A robust optimization framework must account for this. The final optimized layout should be validated not only against the historical 100-year storm but also against a future, climate-adjusted scenario (e.g., historical 100-year intensity + 20-30%). This ensures the spatial plan is resilient to future climate realities.

Part 6: A Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) Framework for Spatial Planning

This section defines the mathematical problem, translating the competing goals of spatial planning into a language that an Evolutionary Algorithm (EA), such as the widely-used NSGA-II (Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II) 90, can solve.

6.1 Formulating the Problem


6.1.1 Decision Variables (The "Genes")

The EA manipulates a "genome" that represents the spatial plan. For the 500x500m site (assuming a 5m DEM resolution, this is a 100x100 grid), the decision variables $x$ are:
	•	$x_{\text{buildings}}$: A $100 \times 100$ binary raster, where $1$ = Building Footprint and $0$ = No Building.
	•	$x_{\text{GI}}$: A $100 \times 100$ integer raster, where $0$ = None, $1$ = Bioswale, $2$ = Permeable Pavement, $3$ = Rain Garden, etc.

6.1.2 Objective Functions (The "Fitness")

The EA evaluates each proposed layout $x$ against three conflicting objectives:
	•	Objective 1: Minimize Flood Risk ($f_1(x)$) 93 This function uses the fast HAND proxy (Part 3.1) to penalize building placement in inundated areas.  $$f_1(x) = \min \left( \sum_{i \in \text{cells}} \text{Depth}_{\text{inundation}, i} \cdot x_{\text{buildings}, i} \right)$$  Where $\text{Depth}_{\text{inundation}, i} = \max(0, (\text{Flood Stage}(x) - \text{HAND}_i))$
	•	Objective 2: Minimize GI/LID Cost ($f_2(x)$) 95 This function penalizes solutions that are expensive, summing the cost of the proposed GI network.  $$f_2(x) = \min \left( \sum_{i \in \text{cells}} \text{Cost}(x_{\text{GI}, i}) \right)$$
	•	Objective 3: Maximize Development Density ($f_3(x)$) 90 This function rewards layouts that achieve the primary economic goal of development.  $$f_3(x) = \max \left( \sum_{i \in \text{cells}} x_{\text{buildings}, i} \right)$$

6.1.3 Constraints (The "Rules")

Constraints are "pass/fail" conditions that define a feasible solution. Any layout $x$ that violates a constraint is heavily penalized or discarded.
	•	Constraint 1: Regulatory Retention ($g_1(x) \ge 0$) Based on the 2017 Turkish regulation 9 and the 25mm first flush standard.10 $$g_1(x) = V_{\text{GI, total}}(x) - V_{\text{runoff, 25mm}}(x) \ge 0$$  Where $V_{\text{GI, total}}$ is the total storage volume provided by the $x_{\text{GI}}$ layout, and $V_{\text{runoff, 25mm}}$ is the runoff volume calculated using the SCS-CN method (Part 1.1.2) for a 25mm storm ($P=25$) on the post-development layout.
	•	Constraint 2: Spatial Constraints ($g_2(x)$) This enforces "no-build" zones.46 Buildings cannot be placed in pre-defined high-risk areas (e.g., the 10-year floodplain, existing river channels, or utility easements). $x_{\text{buildings}, i} = 0$ for all $i \in \text{No-Build Zone}$

6.2 Solving: The Pareto Front and Trade-Offs

A Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) algorithm does not produce a single "best" solution. Instead, it produces a Pareto Front: a set of solutions that are all "non-dominated," meaning no solution is worse than another in all objectives.96
This front visualizes the fundamental trade-offs for the planner.97 By plotting the solutions on a 3D scatter plot (Risk vs. Cost vs. Density), a planner can quantitatively answer questions like:
	•	"What is the minimum cost ($f_2$) to achieve our target density ($f_3$) while keeping risk ($f_1$) below a certain threshold?"
	•	"How much density ($f_3$) must we sacrifice to achieve a 50% reduction in risk ($f_1$)?"
This directly confronts the "Safe Development Paradox".89 It moves the discussion from a vague conflict between safety and development 98 to a quantitative negotiation based on a set of optimized, feasible, and pre-vetted spatial plans.

7.2 High-Fidelity Validation Workflow (The "Turkish Triad")

The fast proxy model is an approximation. To ensure its results are scientifically defensible, a three-step validation against high-fidelity models and real-world events is required.

7.2.1 Step 1: Calibrate High-Fidelity Model (SWMM/HEC-RAS)

	•	Tool: pyswmm 15 or HEC-RAS.99
	•	Method: A detailed SWMM model is built for the Ayamama watershed (or a similar validation site).
	•	Validation Event: The September 2009 Ayamama flood.
	•	Target: The model parameters (e.g., roughness, infiltration) are calibrated until the simulation accurately reproduces the observed outcomes of the 2009 flood: specifically, the ~11 meter flood depths and 1,200 meter inundation extent documented in post-flood analysis.80

7.2.2 Step 2: Validate the Fast Proxy (HAND)

	•	Method: The calibrated high-fidelity model (from Step 1) is now run using the 100-year design storm to generate a "ground truth" 2D inundation depth map ($Map_{HF}$). This $Map_{HF}$ is compared to the inundation map generated by the fast proxy model ($Map_{FP}$), which uses the HAND raster and the synthetic rating curve.
	•	Comparison: The spatial fit of the two maps is quantified. Studies comparing low-complexity models (like HAND) to HEC-RAS 2D have found them to be "reasonably good" 34, though they can overestimate depth 34 or underestimate extent.34 This step quantifies the error and bias of the fast fitness function, which is critical for understanding the final optimization results.

7.2.3 Step 3: Validate the Final Optimized Solution

	•	Method:
	•	The full MOO (Part 6) is run using the fast HAND proxy (Part 7.1) to generate a Pareto front of optimal layouts.
	•	A preferred solution (e.g., a "balanced" design) is selected from this front.
	•	This specific layout (building footprints, GI locations) is then built in the slow, calibrated SWMM model (using pyswmm to modify the model's subcatchments and LID controls 17).
	•	The 100-year design storm is simulated in SWMM on this final, optimized layout.
	•	Validation: The results are checked against two benchmarks:
	•	Does the SWMM simulation confirm the low flood risk predicted by the fast proxy?
	•	Does the solution meet or exceed the performance benchmarks for LID in that watershed? For Ayamama, the target is the 56.02% peak runoff reduction for a 10-year storm achieved by LID combinations in the 2020 study.32 This final step closes the loop, confirming that an optimized design selected via a fast proxy holds up under a rigorous, high-fidelity simulation.

Part 8: State-of-the-Art (2020-2025) and Future Directions

The field of flood modeling and optimization is advancing rapidly, driven by machine learning and a focus on climate adaptation.

8.1 Advances in ML for Flood Prediction

The use of Deep Learning (DL) for rapid flood mapping is a primary research frontier.100 The most promising approach involves using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to learn the complex spatial relationships of flooding.57 Researchers are training CNNs (often U-Net architectures 58) using synthetic training data: the inputs are spatial rasters (DEM, land use, rainfall), and the "ground truth" outputs are flood depth maps generated by thousands of 2D hydrodynamic model (e.g., LISFLOOD-FP) runs.56 This creates a surrogate model (as in Part 3.2) that can predict a full 2D flood map in seconds.58
Research from this period also refines the data inputs: Digital Terrain Models (DTMs), which represent the bare earth, are shown to outperform Digital Surface Models (DSMs), which include buildings and trees.56 While higher-resolution DEMs (e.g., 15m) improve accuracy, coarser DEMs (e.g., 30m) are still considered highly viable for rapid prediction, especially in data-scarce regions.56

8.2 Optimization of Green Infrastructure Networks

Recent research focuses on tightly coupling hydraulic models with optimization algorithms. Studies from 2020-2025 describe frameworks that link SWMM directly to NSGA-II to optimize GI/LID placement for minimizing runoff and cost.91 One such study details a "Cost Optimization Framework for Implementing blue-Green infrastructure (CONFIGURE)" which integrates a high-resolution hydrodynamic model with NSGA-II to find the best cost-benefit trade-offs.104 This is a move toward more computationally intensive but accurate optimization, often aided by GIS-based pre-analysis to identify priority zones for GI placement, thereby reducing the optimizer's search space.105

8.3 Climate-Adaptive Stormwater Design and Nature-based Solutions (NbS)

There is a strong consensus that "climate-adaptive" design is mandatory. This involves acknowledging that climate change renders historical rainfall data non-stationary and insufficient for future design.106 State-of-the-art practice involves using downscaled precipitation data from CMIP6 climate models (e.g., SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5 scenarios) to drive hydrological models, ensuring the designs are resilient to future, more intense rainfall.103
This has led to a focus on Nature-based Solutions (NbS)—a broad category including GI, constructed wetlands, and floodplain restoration.108 A significant body of research (2020-2025) has focused on quantifying NbS effectiveness. The consensus finding is that NbS are highly effective at mitigating frequent, smaller flood events (like the 25mm first flush) and providing co-benefits (water quality, habitat).108 However, their impact on extreme, rare events (like the 100-year flood) is often marginal unless implemented at a massive catchment scale.110
This finding leads to a critical conclusion for resilient spatial planning: a hybrid "Blue-Green-Grey" 112 strategy is required. The optimization framework should use Green Infrastructure (GI/NbS) to meet regulatory and water quality goals (the 25mm storm). It must simultaneously use Spatial Planning (Avoidance)—by optimizing building placement away from high-risk HAND zones—and Grey Infrastructure (Detention) to manage the extreme 100-year flood. No single solution is sufficient; resilience is achieved through this optimized, multi-layered approach.
